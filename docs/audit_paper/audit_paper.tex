\begin{abstract}
We report a systematic, agent-driven audit and lap-time improvement pass
over AtlasAutoware, an open-source ROS~2 racing stack for 1/10-scale
(F1TENTH/RoboRacer) autonomous racing.  Four parallel audit agents
reviewed the stack's subsystems, producing ranked findings that were
independently re-verified and either fixed or rejected; two further
implementation agents then applied current racing research under a hard
evidence bar (a shared deterministic closed-loop benchmark plus
regression tests).  Three results stand out.  (1)~\emph{Feasibility
before speed}: the shipped competition raceline penetrates mapped walls
in five corner regions (minimum clearance $0.000\,\mathrm{m}$), and the
stack's wall-blind corridor refinement deepens the violations --- its
advertised lap-time gains were partly fictional.  A safety-gated
minimum-curvature refinement with a spatially-varying corridor
\cite{heilmeier,tumftm} produces a line with a guaranteed
$0.206\,\mathrm{m}$ clearance floor that is nonetheless
$0.98\,\mathrm{s}$ \emph{faster} closed-loop ($41.48 \rightarrow
40.50\,\mathrm{s}$).  (2)~\emph{Latency is a lap-time problem}: under
$140\,\mathrm{ms}$ of injected actuator delay the model-predictive
controller's lap degrades from $41.5$ to $69.5\,\mathrm{s}$, and the
stack's shipped delay compensation made it \emph{worse} at realistic
latencies because it forward-simulated the newest command while the
actuator was still executing older in-flight commands; a pipeline-aware
predictor holds lap time and tracking at zero-delay levels across the
full $0$--$140\,\mathrm{ms}$ sweep.

Alongside, the audit fixed eight verified defects (three
safety-relevant) and removed $0.12\,\mathrm{rad}$ steering
discontinuities from the pursuit fallback.  Every adopted change is
gated on the shared benchmark; rejected hypotheses are reported with
data.  The test suite grew from 52 to %%NTESTS%% passing tests.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

AtlasAutoware is an integrated F1TENTH racing stack: a simulation bridge
over the \texttt{f1tenth\_gym} simulator \cite{okelly}, SLAM mapping,
minimum-curvature raceline optimization with friction-limited velocity
profiling \cite{heilmeier}, a linear-time-varying MPC raceline tracker
with an OSQP backend, a tire-aware Model- and Acceleration-based Pursuit
(MAP) fallback controller \cite{becker}, a lidar/camera opponent tracker
feeding a race-strategy state machine, and a hardware abstraction layer
(VESC UART or PCA9685 PWM actuation, RPLidar, OAK-D camera).  The same
nodes drive both the simulator and the physical car.

This paper documents one complete
\emph{audit--verify--improve--measure} cycle over that stack with an
explicit objective set by the maintainer: \textbf{reduce lap time, using
current research}.  The contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item a methodology for parallel agent audits and research transfer
        with an explicit evidence bar (Section~\ref{sec:method});
  \item a feasibility analysis of the stack's racing line showing the
        shipped line and its refinement benchmark cut walls, plus a
        safety-gated refinement that is both feasible and faster
        (Section~\ref{sec:raceline});
  \item a root-cause analysis and fix of actuator-delay compensation,
        recovering up to $28\,\mathrm{s}$ per lap at high latency
        (Section~\ref{sec:delay});
  \item an implementation and honest evaluation of contouring control
        (MPCC \cite{liniger,cimpcc}) against the stack's tracking MPC
        under equal physics budgets (Section~\ref{sec:mpcc});
  \item eight verified and fixed defects, three safety-relevant
        (Section~\ref{sec:bugs}); and
  \item negative results --- audit claims and improvement hypotheses
        that did \emph{not} survive verification
        (Section~\ref{sec:negative}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related work and method selection}
\label{sec:related}

The optimization-based racing pipeline --- global raceline from
minimum-curvature QP \cite{heilmeier,tumftm}, friction-limited
forward--backward velocity profile, and a tracking or contouring
controller --- remains the backbone of competitive 1/10-scale racing
\cite{forzaeth,survey}.  Within that family, Model Predictive Contouring
Control \cite{liniger} replaces profile tracking with online progress
maximization over the track corridor; a 2025 curvature-integrated
variant reports 11--12\% lap-time reductions on the F1Tenth platform
\cite{cimpcc}, and the survey of \cite{survey} ranks
optimization-and-tracking pipelines fastest among classical methods.
Learning-based alternatives --- learning MPC \cite{lmpc}, data-driven
tuning and racing-line synthesis \cite{bayesrace}, and most recently
end-to-end reinforcement-learning policies that beat both a
state-of-the-art MPC and expert human drivers on F1Tenth hardware
\cite{rlracing} --- define the current frontier \cite{roboracer}.

Method selection for this pass was constrained by verifiability: every
adopted change must be validated in the repository's deterministic
closed-loop harness inside the development sandbox (no GPU training, no
live simulator).  We therefore implemented the strongest
\emph{verifiable} items --- safety-gated minimum-curvature refinement
\cite{heilmeier}, pipeline-aware delay compensation, and a
curvature-integrated kinematic MPCC \cite{liniger,cimpcc} evaluated
under a self-enforced friction budget --- and scope RL policy training
\cite{rlracing} as future work on the real simulator.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Parallel subsystem audits}

Four read-only audit agents were launched concurrently, one per
subsystem, instructed to rank findings by impact~$\times$~confidence and
to verify suspicions by executing the stack's pure-logic paths (the
repository isolates its mathematics from ROS, so controllers,
estimators, planners, and protocol codecs all run under plain
\texttt{pytest}): \textbf{control} (LTV-MPC, its ROS wrapper with AEB
and traction governor, MAP fallback, pure pursuit);
\textbf{estimation and planning} (velocity EKF, lidar de-skew, velocity
profiler, raceline optimizer/refiner, overtaking spliner);
\textbf{strategy and simulation bridge} (opponent tracker and fusion,
strategy state machine, two-car gym bridge); and \textbf{hardware and
perception} (drive node, VESC UART protocol, PCA9685 driver, RPLidar
binning, camera geometry).

\subsection{Evidence bar}

Audit findings are treated as \emph{hypotheses}.  Each was re-verified
against the source by the coordinating agent before any code change;
claims that could not be reproduced were rejected
(Section~\ref{sec:negative}).  Behavioural changes had to satisfy two
gates:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Regression gate}: the full unit/closed-loop test suite
        passes, and each fixed defect gains a test that fails on the old
        code wherever the logic is hardware-independent.
  \item \textbf{Benchmark gate}: performance-motivated changes must
        improve the shared deterministic closed-loop lap benchmark and
        may not regress the zero-delay baseline.  For raceline changes
        a third gate applies: a \emph{clearance floor} against the
        occupancy grid's euclidean distance field.
\end{enumerate}
Verified single-site defects were fixed by the coordinating agent;
larger work items went to parallel implementation subagents with
strictly disjoint file ownership, and their claims (including
``adopted'' vs.\ ``rejected'' calls) were re-run and re-verified by the
coordinator before committing.

\subsection{The shared closed-loop harness}
\label{sec:harness}

All quantitative claims use the repository's single benchmark
(\texttt{tests/closed\_loop.py}): a kinematic-bicycle plant integrated
at $50\,\mathrm{Hz}$ lapping the competition raceline, starting from a
deliberate $0.36\,\mathrm{m}$ pose error.  The harness reports lap time
and perpendicular cross-track error (XTE; mean and max after a settle
period), and can hold each command in a FIFO to emulate
sensor-to-actuator latency.  The plant matches the model the MPC plans
with, so results isolate \emph{controller and line} behaviour from model
error; runs are deterministic and free of ROS scheduling noise.  Because
the plant has no grip limit, any controller allowed to choose its own
speeds must \emph{self-enforce} the friction budget
(Section~\ref{sec:mpcc}); XTE differences below
${\sim}0.005\,\mathrm{m}$ are within discretization noise and are not
claimed as improvements.

\section{Lap time I: the racing line must first exist on the track}
\label{sec:raceline}

\subsection{The shipped line cuts walls}

Checking the competition raceline against the track map's euclidean
distance field (EDT of the occupancy grid, $0.1296\,\mathrm{m}$/px)
shows its \textbf{minimum wall clearance is} \m{0.000}: in five corner
regions the line passes \emph{inside} mapped walls, up to \m{0.518}
deep at the worst chicane (Figure~\ref{fig:raceline}).  The 5th
percentile of clearance is \m{0.149} --- less than half a car width
from the wall for a tenth of the lap.  Worse, the repository's
refinement benchmark applies a \emph{scalar} corridor with no wall
knowledge; at every corridor setting in $\{0.10\ldots0.35\}\,\mathrm{m}$
the refined line's minimum clearance stays \m{0.000} (5th percentile
collapsing to \m{0.001} at $0.35$): the benchmark's advertised lap-time
gains (up to $5\,\mathrm{s}$) come from cutting the same walls deeper.
Closed-loop lap times on such lines are partly fictional --- a real car
DNFs there.

\subsection{Safety-gated minimum-curvature refinement}

The replacement pipeline (\texttt{tools/refine\_comp\_raceline.py},
deterministic; re-run byte-identical) is the Heilmeier
minimum-curvature QP \cite{heilmeier,tumftm} made wall-aware:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{safety repair}: waypoints below the clearance floor are
        moved minimally toward open space along the track normal;
  \item \textbf{refinement with a spatially-varying corridor}: the
        existing QP refiner natively accepts per-point corridor
        budgets; each point's budget is
        $\mathrm{clip}\!\left(\min(c_i - 0.20, \; 0.35 - d_i),\,
        0,\, 0.35\right)$ where $c_i$ is current clearance and $d_i$
        displacement already spent --- so the optimizer can never buy
        curvature with clearance below the \m{0.20} floor;
  \item \textbf{re-profiling} with the \emph{same} friction parameters
        as the shipped line ($a_{\mathrm{lat}} \le 6.5\,\mathrm{m/s^2}$,
        $a_{\mathrm{accel}} \le 5.0$, $a_{\mathrm{brake}} \le 7.0$,
        $v \in [1.5, 7.0]\,\mathrm{m/s}$; the shipped speed column is
        reproduced to $<10^{-3}\,\mathrm{m/s}$ by these parameters, so
        no limit was raised).
\end{enumerate}

The result (Table~\ref{tab:raceline}) inverts the usual
speed--safety trade: enforcing feasibility \emph{and} refining curvature
yields $-0.98\,\mathrm{s}$ closed-loop ($41.48 \rightarrow
40.50\,\mathrm{s}$) with maximum curvature reduced $2.4\times$ --- the
gains previously attributed to corridor width were actually available
from geometry, without the wall cutting.

\section{Lap time II: latency-robust control}
\label{sec:delay}

A real car has $50$--$150\,\mathrm{ms}$ of sensor-to-actuator latency.
The baseline sweep (Table~\ref{tab:delay}, ``naive'') shows the MPC ---
\m{0.123} mean XTE at zero delay --- degrading $4.8\times$ by \ms{140},
with lap time inflating from $41.5$ to $69.5\,\mathrm{s}$: at race
latencies, delay handling \emph{is} a lap-time feature.

The stack already shipped a compensator: \texttt{predict\_state}
forward-integrates the kinematic bicycle by the known delay and the MPC
solves from the predicted pose.  Benchmarked closed-loop, the shipped
compensation was \emph{worse than no compensation} at \ms{80} (XTE mean
$0.242$ vs.\ $0.195$) and unstable beyond \ms{100}, producing
corner-cutting laps.  The root cause is the prediction's command
semantics: it held the \emph{newest} commanded (steer, speed) constant
over the whole delay window, but under pipeline latency the actuator is
still executing the \emph{older in-flight} commands during that window.
Whenever steering is changing --- every corner entry and exit --- the
prediction over-rotates the pose, and the error grows with delay until
the loop destabilizes.

The fix keeps a buffer of the commands published during the last delay
window and integrates the prediction through them oldest-first, each
holding its slice of the window (scalar inputs keep bit-identical
legacy behaviour).  Tracking then holds at zero-delay levels --- XTE
mean $0.121$--$0.123\,\mathrm{m}$, lap time within $0.12\,\mathrm{s}$ of
baseline --- across the entire sweep (Table~\ref{tab:delay},
Figure~\ref{fig:delay}); at \ms{140} that is $27.9\,\mathrm{s}$ of lap
time recovered relative to naive.  The property carries to the new
raceline of Section~\ref{sec:raceline}: at \ms{100}, $40.56\,\mathrm{s}$
compensated vs.\ $50.06\,\mathrm{s}$ naive.  A regression test gates
the \ms{100} case.

\section{Supporting result: smooth fallback steering}
\label{sec:map}

The MAP fallback \cite{becker} inverts a lateral-acceleration
$\to$ steer lookup table.  The lateral-acceleration axis was
interpolated but the speed axis selected the \emph{nearest} LUT column,
so commanded steering jumped by up to $0.119\,\mathrm{rad}$
($\approx 6.8^{\circ}$) at every column midpoint
(Figure~\ref{fig:lut}).  The fix inverts the two bracketing speed
columns and blends linearly --- bit-identical at the LUT's sample
speeds, clamped outside the table, and cheaper per call
($\approx 5\,\mu\mathrm{s}$).  Closed-loop the change is
neutral-to-slightly-better (at $1.3\times$ profile speed: XTE mean
$0.1575 \rightarrow 0.1564$, max $1.287 \rightarrow 1.267$,
steering-rate std $1.144 \rightarrow 1.104\,\mathrm{rad/s}$); it was
adopted for the open-loop continuity, which matters on the physical car
where each discontinuity excites the steering servo.

\section{Verified defects and fixes}
\label{sec:bugs}

Table~\ref{tab:bugs} summarises the eight verified defects fixed during
the audit.  Three are safety-relevant: the strategist's lateral-offset
clamps used \emph{swapped wall bounds} at all four decision sites (a
$+$left offset was clamped by the \emph{right} wall's free room --- with
a wall \m{0.1} to the left, a DEFEND decision would command \m{0.35}
left, i.e.\ \m{0.25} into the wall); the EVADE mode \emph{honoured a
stale committed pass side} and could steer into the car that was passing
(fixed to always move away and clear the commitment, with a two-phase
regression scenario); and the two-car simulation bridge \emph{froze
physics} whenever the opponent driver had not published, while sensors
kept republishing the stale world --- the ego then raced a ghost car
(fixed to step on ego publish with the opponent's last-known command).

\section{Negative results}
\label{sec:negative}

Findings that did not survive verification, recorded so they are not
re-raised:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The refinement benchmark's lap-time gains were partly
        wall-cutting.}  The repository's prior evaluation credited
        corridor refinement with up to $5\,\mathrm{s}$/lap; against the
        map's distance field those refined lines have \m{0.000} minimum
        clearance.  The honest, wall-aware gain at a \m{0.20} clearance
        floor is $0.98\,\mathrm{s}$ (Section~\ref{sec:raceline}).
  \item \textbf{Estimation and planning are clean.}  The audit of the
        velocity EKF (Jacobians, Joseph-form covariance update, slip
        gating), lidar de-skew geometry, friction-ellipse
        forward--backward profiler (including lap-seam propagation),
        the optimizer/refiner QP formulations, and the Frenet spliner
        found no correctness defects; the only action was a cosmetic
        NaN-warning fix.
  \item \textbf{MPC formulation is sound.}  Discrete linearization,
        angle unwrapping, OSQP warm-start usage, and the persistent-QP
        sparsity-update path were checked and confirmed correct.
  \item \textbf{``Nearest index not recomputed after prediction'' ---
        false.}  The audit hypothesized the ROS node fed the MPC a
        nearest-raceline index from the stale pose; inspection showed it
        already recomputes it from the predicted pose.  The delay defect
        was the command-pipeline semantics of Section~\ref{sec:delay}.
  \item \textbf{``Reference temporal mismatch under delay'' --- rejected
        empirically.}  After the pipeline fix, residual compensated XTE
        ($0.121$) matches the zero-delay baseline ($0.123$), leaving
        nothing for a reference-time shift to explain.
  \item \textbf{Curvature-scaled speed weighting --- rejected.}
        Scaling the MPC's speed-tracking weight by reference curvature,
        $q_v\,(1 + k\,|\kappa_{\mathrm{ref}}|)$, swept over
        $k \in \{0.5, 1, 2, 4\}$: XTE mean rose monotonically
        ($0.123 \rightarrow 0.125$--$0.134$) with no
        $\mathrm{XTE}_{\max}$ improvement at any gain.  On a kinematic
        plant with no grip limit, harder speed tracking only trades away
        position tracking.  Reverted; recorded in \texttt{docs/mpc.md}.
  \item \textbf{AEB time-to-collision is ego-speed based.}  Real
        limitation (head-on closing speed underestimated), but it is
        the standard single-lidar formulation; a proper fix needs
        opponent velocity fused into the AEB path --- scoped as future
        work rather than a landed half-measure.
\end{itemize}

\section{Threats to validity}

The harness plant is the kinematic bicycle the MPC plans with: results
isolate controller and line behaviour but exclude tire slip, which on
the real car is handled by the traction governor and slip-rejecting EKF
(audited clean, unchanged).  Controllers that choose their own speeds
self-enforce the friction budget (Section~\ref{sec:mpcc}); the budget
itself ($6.5\,\mathrm{m/s^2}$) was inherited, not re-identified.  Delay
compensation is evaluated with the compensator told the true delay; on
hardware the delay must be identified
(\texttt{actuation\_delay} parameter), and sensitivity to
misidentification is not characterized here.  Wall clearance is judged
against the SLAM map; map error consumes part of the \m{0.20} floor.
Strategy fixes are validated by unit-level scenario tests, not full
two-car simulation.

\section{Conclusion}

Asked to make a racing stack faster with current research, the most
valuable early move turned out to be making its existing speed
\emph{real}: the shipped racing line --- and the benchmark used to
improve it --- cut walls, and the controller's deployed latency path was
benchmarked only at zero delay, where its broken compensation was
invisible.  After repairing feasibility, the research transfer paid in
the expected order: wall-aware minimum-curvature refinement
\cite{heilmeier} ($-0.98\,\mathrm{s}$, with a clearance guarantee the
old line never had), pipeline-aware delay compensation (up to
$-27.9\,\mathrm{s}$ at \ms{140} vs.\ naive; the difference between a
competitive lap and a crawl at real latencies)%%CONCL_MPCC%%.
Of the improvement hypotheses tested, the adopted ones are exactly
those that survived a deterministic closed-loop benchmark with hard
safety gates; the rejected ones are reported with data.  The general
lesson for racing stacks: \emph{a lap time is only as real as the
feasibility and latency assumptions under it} --- benchmark the
deployed path, including the actuator pipeline and the walls.
